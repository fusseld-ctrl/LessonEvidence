Rights within Reach: The Bill of Rights

Your mission is to turn constitutional protections into clear, usable tools for real people in North Carolina.

Quick norms
  • Be accurate.
  • Be respectful.
  • Be useful to your audience.
🎯
Essential Question

How can we translate complex constitutional protections into accessible tools that address the specific vulnerabilities of people groups in North Carolina?

✅
Learning Objectives

By the end of this project, you should be able to do the following:

  1. Define and distinguish civil liberties and civil rights, and explain why both matter in a democratic society.
  2. Explain due process and describe how the Bill of Rights protects individuals during government action (especially in policing and courts).
  3. Identify vulnerable groups and real situations where people may not understand their rights, then connect those situations to specific amendments.
  4. Evaluate scenarios to determine whether a right was protected or violated, using evidence and clear constitutional reasoning.
  5. Create an accessible public tool that helps a specific audience understand and use Bill of Rights protections in real life.
  6. Extension: Judge effectiveness of the U.S. legal system in protecting freedom, equality, and justice by supporting your claim with evidence.
📌
Key Vocabulary
Civil Liberties
Protections from government interference, especially freedoms like speech, religion, press, assembly, and privacy.
Civil Rights
Protections against unfair treatment and discrimination, ensuring equal access and equal protection under the law.
Due Process
A guarantee that the government must follow fair procedures and respect legal rights before taking life, liberty, or property.
Bill of Rights
The first ten amendments to the U.S. Constitution that protect individual freedoms and rights.
Individual Rights
Freedoms and protections held by each person, especially when interacting with government authority.
Warrant
A court-issued order allowing law enforcement to search or seize, usually requiring a strong factual basis.
Probable Cause
Reasonable evidence-based belief that a crime occurred or that evidence will be found in a specific place.
Enumeration
A listed set of rights or powers. In the Bill of Rights, specific rights are named, but not all rights are listed (see the 9th Amendment idea).

Your Role and Task
You are Public Interest Media Strategists

You have been commissioned by a local Legal Aid non-profit. They have noticed a spike in vulnerable groups interacting with law enforcement without understanding their rights.

  • Choose a vulnerable group or a focused topic (examples: traffic stops, protests, juvenile detention).
  • Research the relevant Bill of Rights protections and due process expectations.
  • Create an accessible tool that helps your audience understand and apply their rights.
Materials Needed
  • Pocket Constitution
  • Computer
  • Any differentiated materials provided for you or your group
Your Final Product

Format is flexible (infographic, one-pager, video, audio PSA, slide deck, social post carousel, mini-handbook, etc.). Your product must be accurate, audience-friendly, and grounded in the Bill of Rights.

Find the Problem: One Strong Article
Your goal

Find a credible article from the last few years that describes a real event or interaction that could be evidence of a civil liberties or civil rights problem. This article becomes your starting point.

Authority
Who published it? Are they reliable? Do they cite evidence or official details?
Relevance
Does it connect directly to your group or topic and the Bill of Rights?
Specificity
Does it describe what happened, to whom, where, and what the dispute is?
Before you search, I will model how to locate acceptable articles based on topic focus, authority, and relevance.

Create the Tool
Non-negotiables for your product
  • Accurate Bill of Rights connections (amendments and protections)
  • Clear, accessible language for your audience
  • Scenario-based guidance (what to do, what to say, what to ask)
  • Evidence and citations (respect copyright and avoid plagiarism)
Include a reasoning move
Your tool must help the audience think, not just memorize. Include at least one section where you guide them through a decision:
Example: “In this scenario, which right is involved? What should the system do? What would count as a violation?”

Criteria for Success

This is what “good” looks like in this project. Use this as a checklist while you work.

Build strong content knowledge (Individual)
You can accurately explain relevant rights and due process and connect them directly to the situation you studied.
Product quality (Group)
Your tool is clear, functional, and meets the purpose. Content and design support the audience. You built it intentionally, not randomly.
Use technology and media strategically (Group)
Your format makes sense for your audience and strengthens your message instead of distracting from it.
Cultural awareness and perspective (Group)
You show respect and understanding for the people impacted. You avoid stereotypes and consider multiple perspectives.
Use and manage information (Group)
You use credible sources, track them accurately, and include citations that allow others to locate your evidence. Copyright is respected.
Communicate clearly (Individual)
You explain your thinking clearly and confidently, using the Constitution as evidence rather than opinions alone.
Collaborate with others (Individual)
You listen, contribute, stay on task, and support your team through responsible effort and follow-through.
Bottom line
You are not just learning rights as vocabulary. You are learning to apply them to real situations and communicate them in a way that protects people.
