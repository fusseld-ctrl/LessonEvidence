The Industrial Revolution

Essential Question:

Did the Industrial Revolution mark a greater transformation in human society than the Neolithic Revolution?

Overview

Over the next several days, you will explore one of the biggest questions in world history: whether the Industrial Revolution was the greatest transformation in human society since the Neolithic Revolution. You will investigate how industrialization changed the way humans lived and worked, practice evaluating information for reliability, and use your discoveries to prepare for a Socratic Seminar.

Movement 1: Spark Curiosity

Today you’ll begin outside. Watch carefully as your classmates take turns using different tools to cut wood. 

When we return to the classroom, imagine a world where no machines exist — no engines, no electricity, no powered equipment. How would this change the way people live and work?

Inquiry Journal Prompts:
  • What did you notice about how tools improved efficiency?
  • How do you think technology changes human life?
  • What curiosities or “I wonder…” questions do you have after this experience?

Movement 2: Unpacking the Essential Question

Read our essential question again. In your inquiry journal, describe what you think it means and what you expect to discover as we explore this topic.

After writing, meet with a partner for a collaborative discussion. Practice paraphrasing what your partner says before you add your own thoughts.

Partner Discussion Questions (scaffolds if you need them):
  1. What do you think the essential question is really asking us to figure out?
  2. What do you already know about how humans lived before modern machines?
  3. What do you think we might discover about the positives and negatives of change?
  4. Why might historians compare the Industrial Revolution to the Neolithic Revolution?

Working individually, choose an AI (like ChatGPT, Gemini, or Perplexity) to begin researching your questions. Record what you discover and make sure to capture which question each answer connects to. You can use your inquiry journal or a digital document.

Movement 5: Partner Dialogue & Comparison

Find a partner from another group. Share the questions your teams created and compare your lists. Discuss which questions overlap and which differ. When differences appear, ask: Is this question worth keeping? Why or why not?

Use dialogue frames from the wall to guide your conversation. No writing yet — just talk, compare, and share what you’ve learned so far.

Movement 6: Evaluating Sources with the CRAAP Test

You’ll learn how to use the CRAAP Test to evaluate online sources for credibility. Your teacher will model how to analyze a website for: Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, and Purpose.

Remember: you don’t need to answer every question on the CRAAP Test — just enough to feel confident that your source is reliable.

Movement 7: Corroborating with Human Sources

Working with your partner, you will find four reputable online sources that confirm or challenge what you learned from AI. Your goal is to corroborate AI information with human-created evidence while evaluating each source’s reliability using the CRAAP Test.

Follow the steps below carefully. You and your partner will turn in one completed document. Both partners will receive the same classwork grade. You can earn a 100, 95, 90, 80, 70, 60, 50, 25, or 0 based on the rubric.

Steps to Complete:
  1. Choose one of your Costa Questions that you want to verify or expand.
  2. Write a brief summary (1–2 sentences) of what the AI said about that question.
  3. Find a human source that helps answer or confirm this question.
  4. Apply the CRAAP Test using the Yes/No checklist below.
  5. Decide whether you trust this source and whether it supports or contradicts what AI said.
  6. List any of your other Costa Questions that this source also answers.
  7. Repeat for four total sources.
  8. For one source, write a short paragraph (3–4 sentences) explaining why you trust it using the CRAAP results.

Reminder: You do not have to answer “Yes” to every CRAAP question to trust a source. However, most of your answers should show confidence in its reliability.

Movement 8: Forming an Argument

Work with your partner to begin crafting your stance on our essential question: Did the Industrial Revolution mark a greater transformation in human society than the Neolithic Revolution?

Use evidence from both your AI research and your human sources to support your claim.

Inquiry Journal Reflection: What evidence most strongly supports your stance? Where might others disagree with you?

Movement 9: The Urban Game

Play the Urban Game individually. Follow the story instructions carefully as you draw and watch your village evolve through the Industrial Revolution.

Inquiry Journal Reflection:
  • What changes did you notice most clearly during the game?
  • How did life improve — and how did it worsen — for people in industrial towns?

Movement 10: Preparing for the Socratic Seminar

Use your inquiry journal to collect key ideas, evidence, and questions that will help you in the Socratic Seminar. You will each contribute by posing questions, sharing insights, or defending a stance.

Movement 11: The Socratic Seminar

A Socratic Seminar is a structured discussion in which participants use evidence, reasoning, and respectful dialogue to explore complex ideas. You are not debating to “win” — you are engaging to understand.

Your Role:

  • Pose thoughtful questions related to our essential question.
  • Respond with evidence from your research or the Urban Game.
  • Paraphrase others before responding.
  • Build on ideas and make connections between viewpoints.

Your participation grade will reflect how well you listen, engage, and use evidence, not just how often you speak.
