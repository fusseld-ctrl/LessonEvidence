The Treaty of Versailles

on Trial

Essential Question

Should the Treaty of Versailles' terms for Germany be overturned?

Key Terms to Know

These eight terms are mission critical to everything we are doing this unit. Before we go a single day further, make sure you can define each one in your own words.

Treaty of Versailles

The 1919 peace agreement that formally ended World War I. Negotiated primarily by the Allied Powers without German participation, it imposed sweeping penalties on Germany including territorial losses, military restrictions, and financial reparations.

War Guilt Clause

Article 231 of the Treaty of Versailles, which forced Germany to accept full and sole responsibility for causing World War I. This clause was used to justify every financial and territorial penalty placed on Germany.

Reparations

Financial payments demanded of a defeated nation to compensate the victors for war damages. Germany was ordered to pay 132 billion gold marks -- a sum so large it was not fully paid off until 2010.

Militarism

The belief that a nation should maintain a strong military capability and be prepared to use it aggressively. In pre-WWI Europe, militarism drove an arms race among the major powers that made large-scale war far more likely once the spark was struck.

Nationalism

Intense loyalty and devotion to one's nation or ethnic group, often at the expense of others. In the early 20th century, nationalist movements -- especially in the Balkans -- destabilized empires and created the conditions for conflict.

Imperialism

The policy of extending a nation's power through colonization, military force, or economic dominance. European competition for colonies and resources created deep rivalries that fed directly into the tensions of 1914.

Alliances

Formal agreements between nations to support one another, especially in times of war. By 1914, Europe had divided into two armed camps -- the Triple Entente and the Triple Alliance -- meaning a conflict between two nations could drag in all of them.

Permanent Court of International Justice

Established in 1920 as part of the League of Nations, the PCIJ was the world's first permanent international tribunal for resolving disputes between nations through law rather than warfare. It is the predecessor to today's International Court of Justice.

Day Two

The War's End and the Simulation Begins

We will finish our walk through the notes today, covering the end of the war, the armistice, and the events that led to the peace negotiations at Paris. Then we step out of the history classroom and into the courtroom.

Dispatch from the Court  |  The Hague, Netherlands  |  1922

The Great War is over. The guns fell silent on November 11, 1918, and after months of negotiation at the Paris Peace Conference, the Allied Powers -- led by France, Great Britain, and the United States -- presented Germany with the terms of peace. Germany had no seat at the table. No voice in the negotiations. No opportunity to argue its case.

What Germany received instead was the Treaty of Versailles. It stripped Germany of 13% of its territory and 10% of its population. It dismantled the German military, capping its army at 100,000 soldiers and banning submarines, aircraft, and tanks. It forced Germany to formally accept full and sole responsibility for the war under Article 231 -- a clause that became known simply as the War Guilt Clause. And it ordered Germany to pay reparations so enormous that economists warned the payments could destabilize the entire European economy.

Three years have now passed. The German economy is in freefall. Unemployment is rising. Resentment is burning. And a group of young attorneys, acting on behalf of the German people, has done something unprecedented -- they have petitioned the newly established Permanent Court of International Justice to hear a challenge to the treaty's most punishing provisions. Their argument is straightforward: the terms are excessive, the process was unjust, and the punishment does not fit the evidence.

In response, a coalition of attorneys representing France, Great Britain, and the United States has assembled to defend the treaty. Their position is equally clear: Germany started this war. Germany must pay for it. The terms of the treaty are not only justified -- they are necessary to ensure that nothing like the Great War ever happens again.

The Court Has Been Convened. Your Role:
Petitioners

You represent the German people. Your task is to challenge the treaty's harshest provisions, arguing that the terms were imposed without Germany's input, assign blame Germany does not fully deserve, and have produced conditions of undue suffering that threaten the stability of Europe.

Respondents

You represent the Allied Powers. Your task is to defend the treaty in full, arguing that Germany bears responsibility for the war's destruction, that the terms are proportionate to the harm caused, and that any reduction in consequences would be an injustice to the nations that suffered most.

The Justices

Two to three students will serve as justices of the court. You are not on either team. Your responsibility is to listen carefully, ask probing questions of witnesses, and ultimately render a decision based on the strength of the arguments you hear. More on your role in the days ahead.

What Happens Today

After we finish notes, I will assign teams and justices. Within your team, you will need to designate 2-3 attorneys and 5-6 witnesses before you leave today. Attorneys will lead your opening statement, question your own witnesses, and deliver your closing argument. Witnesses will represent nations or other figures who serve as evidence in your case.

Important: Each witness nation may only be called by one side. It is first come, first served -- when Day 3 begins, I will open a brief calling window. Your team should arrive with a ranked list of your top witness choices ready to go.

Wilson's Fourteen Points: What Peace Could Have Looked Like

Before the treaty was written, U.S. President Woodrow Wilson proposed a vision for lasting peace based on fourteen specific principles. Germany had actually hoped these points would form the basis for any peace agreement. They did not. Understanding the gap between what Wilson proposed and what the treaty actually delivered is one of the most important contrasts in this case.

The Fourteen Points -- Summary
1. Open diplomacy -- no secret treaties
2. Freedom of the seas for all nations
3. Removal of economic trade barriers
4. Reduction of armaments to the lowest level consistent with domestic safety
5. Impartial adjustment of colonial claims
6. Evacuation of Russian territory
7. Restoration and independence of Belgium
8. Return of Alsace-Lorraine to France
9. Readjustment of Italian borders along ethnic lines
10. Autonomy for the peoples of Austria-Hungary
11. Restoration of the Balkan nations
12. Self-determination for peoples under Ottoman rule
13. Independent Polish state with access to the sea
14. Formation of a general association of nations to guarantee independence and territorial integrity for all states (the League of Nations)

As you read the treaty terms, ask yourself: which of these principles survived in the final agreement, and which were abandoned? That gap is evidence.

Step 2: Your Role Takes Over
Petitioners & Respondents
Claim Your Witnesses

At the start of today's work time, I will open a brief window for witness claims. Have your ranked list of preferred witness nations ready. Call your top choice aloud -- I will write claimed witnesses on the board. Each witness nation may only be called by one side. First come, first served. Plan your backup choices.

Craft Your Opening Statement

Your opening statement is your team's thesis delivered aloud to the court. It should run 3-4 minutes -- a strong argument earns its full time, and a thin one that wraps up in 90 seconds is a signal that the case needs more development. Your opening must include:

01 Your core argument -- a clear, debatable claim about whether the treaty's terms are just or unjust
02 An introduction of each witness your team will call, with a brief preview of what their testimony will establish
03 A statement connecting your witnesses back to the essential question
Script Your Witness Examinations

Each team may call witnesses. Each examination should run 2-3 minutes and consist of scripted questions from your attorneys and scripted responses from your witnesses. Think carefully about what each witness uniquely contributes -- two sharp, well-supported answers beat five shallow ones. Your witnesses are your evidence. Treat them accordingly.

Find additional sources beyond what I have given you. The documents above are a starting point, not a ceiling.

The Justices

While the teams are building their cases, you are doing your own parallel work. Your job today is to prepare to be the sharpest minds in the room on Day 4.

Read Independently

Read the treaty summary article and review the Fourteen Points summary above carefully and independently. You are not building an argument for either side -- you are building your own informed understanding of the facts so you can evaluate what you hear in court.

Draft Your Bench Brief

In your inquiry journal, draft 3-5 questions you intend to ask witnesses during testimony. These should be genuine cross-examination questions -- ones that probe the strength of a witness's position, not softballs. Also write out what you think strong evidence looks like for each side. What would actually move you?

Set Your Evaluation Standard

Write a brief paragraph describing what a winning argument looks like to you. What kinds of evidence are most convincing? What makes an argument fall apart? You will use this to guide your deliberation on Day 4 and Day 5.

Day Four

The Court Is in Session

Today the simulation runs. The order of proceedings is below. Everyone should arrive ready -- opening statements are scripted, witness examinations are scripted, and the court does not wait for teams that are still organizing themselves when we begin.

Order of Proceedings
~5 min
Court is called to order. Justices take their seats. I introduce the proceedings.
~8 min
Opening statements -- Petitioners present (3-4 min), then Respondents present (3-4 min).
~5 min
Justices may ask one clarifying question of each team before witnesses are called.
~25 min
Witness testimony -- teams alternate calling witnesses (2-3 min each examination). Justices may ask one follow-up question per witness after examination concludes.
~15 min
Teams huddle to collaboratively draft closing arguments. Justices deliberate quietly, recording initial impressions and identifying what they still need to hear.
~2 min
Court recesses. Closing arguments delivered Day 5.
During the Closing Draft Phase
Petitioners & Respondents

Your closing argument should run 3-4 minutes. It must do two things: reaffirm your core argument with your strongest evidence, and directly address at least one thing the other side presented that you want to push back against. A closing that ignores the opposing case is a weak closing.

All team members contribute to drafting. Your attorneys will deliver it, but the argument belongs to everyone.

The Justices

While the teams are drafting, you are deliberating. Review your bench brief from Day 3. Write out your current impressions of each side's case -- what was strong, what was unconvincing, what questions remain. You are not rendering a final decision yet. You are organizing your thinking so that after closing arguments tomorrow you can deliberate quickly and confidently.

Write down specifically what you are hoping each team addresses in their closing. That focus will make you a sharper evaluator tomorrow.

Day 4 Reflection

Record your response in your inquiry journal before leaving today.

"After hearing both sides present today, what argument surprised you most or changed how you were thinking? Has your personal position on the essential question shifted at all? What would it take to convince you otherwise?"

Day Five

Closing Arguments, the Decision, and Your Own Voice

Today the court reaches its conclusion -- and then you step out of your assigned role entirely.

Closing Arguments

Both teams deliver their closing arguments (3-4 minutes each). Justices listen and take final notes. No new witnesses may be introduced in closing -- this is your final case, built on what has already been presented.

Justice Deliberation and Decision

The justices deliberate briefly. They will share their decision with the court and offer a brief explanation of what argument or evidence most influenced their ruling. Then I will announce my own decision based on what I observed across both days of proceedings.

Socratic Seminar

You are now free from your assigned position. We return to the essential question as a class: Should the Treaty of Versailles' terms for Germany be overturned? Come ready to argue what you actually believe, supported by what you have learned. This is your chance to abandon your team's position if the evidence has led you somewhere different.
