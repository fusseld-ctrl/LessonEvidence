The Reveal

A Media Literacy Investigation
Essential Question
Who is shaping what you believe, and do you consent to it?

5 Days   |   Individual Investigation   |   Inquiry Notebook Required   |   Day 5: Choose Your Track

Before We Begin

Read This First.

Somewhere in North Carolina right now, two people are reading about the same event.

One is in a suburb outside Charlotte. One is in a small town not far from here. They both saw the same thing happen -- the same vote, the same protest, the same storm, the same shooting. They both went looking for information. They both found it.

But what they found was not the same.

One of them is angry. One of them is relieved. One of them thinks the people in charge handled it well. One of them thinks those same people should be removed from office. They are not arguing. They never met. They just live inside different versions of the same reality -- and neither of them knows it.

Here is the part that should unsettle you: you are one of those people.

Right now, today, without knowing it, you are living inside a version of reality that was built for you. Not by accident. On purpose. By people who studied what makes you click, what makes you angry, what makes you share, what makes you come back. They are very good at their jobs.

Eastern North Carolina has a story. You have lived it or you have heard it -- the hurricanes that hit here harder than anywhere, the farms and factories, the towns that show up in national news when something goes wrong and disappear when the cameras leave. People outside this region have strong opinions about it. They form those opinions from somewhere -- from what they read, what they watch, what their algorithm serves them on a Tuesday afternoon.

Most of them have never been here.

That gap -- between what people consume and what is actually true -- is not a small problem. It shapes elections. It shapes policy. It shapes how much money comes to rebuild a town after a flood and how much does not. It shapes whether the people making decisions about your community ever actually understand it.

Media literacy is not a school skill. It is a survival skill for people who live in places the media gets wrong.

This week, you are going to find out what they built.

And decide whether you consent to living in it.

Movement One
Day 1: Waking Up
70 min
The Goal

Before I (Mr. Fussell) tell you anything about media, you are going to feel something about it. Today is not about notes. Today is about waking up to what is already happening around you.

What Happens Today
Part 1  |  Two Films. One Idea. (15 min)

Mr. Fussell will show you two clips without any introduction. Watch them. Do not talk. When they are done, write one sentence in your inquiry notebook: What do these two things have in common?

Then we will talk about what you saw -- and what it has to do with your phone.

Part 2  |  Welcome to The Reveal (10 min)

Mr. Fussell will introduce this investigation -- what it is, what you will do, and what the next five days look like. You will receive your role as an analyst. This room, for the next five days, is The Reveal -- a media analysis think tank commissioned to investigate one of the most important civic questions of your generation.

Part 3  |  My Media Diet (15 min)

Open your inquiry notebook. This is Day 1 of your Media Audit Log. Answer the following guided questions honestly. No one is judging your media consumption. This is data, not a confession.

  • What are the top three sources where you get information about the world -- news, events, politics, or current events?
  • How many hours per day would you estimate you spend consuming media of any kind?
  • Have you ever changed your mind about something because of something you saw, read, or watched? What happened?
  • Do you think the media you consume is accurate? How do you know?
  • Do you think the media you consume is trying to influence you? Does that bother you?
Part 4  |  Your Investigative Questions (20 min)

Every analyst enters an investigation with questions they genuinely want answered. Now it is your turn. Using Costa's Levels of Questioning, you will write at least two questions that you actually want to investigate this week.

Costa's Level Reminder
Level 1
Found directly in the text. One right answer. Who owns CNN?
Level 2 ✓
Requires analysis across sources. How does ownership shape the stories a network chooses to tell?
Level 3 ✓
Goes beyond the text. Big ideas, civic implications. If I only consume one source, what kind of citizen does that make me?

Write your questions in your inquiry notebook. Label each one Level 2 or Level 3. These are your questions for this investigation. You will return to them on Day 5 and answer them based on what you found.

Before You Leave

Choose the media source you will investigate for this entire unit. It should be something you actually use -- a platform, a channel, a publication, an account, a feed. Bring examples of it to Day 2. This is your beat. You are the analyst assigned to it.

Movement Two
Day 2: The Four Moves
70 min
The Goal

Today you receive your investigative toolkit. These are the four moves every analyst at The Reveal uses to examine any piece of media -- any platform, any era, any story. You will practice them together first, then begin turning them on your own source.

The Analyst's Framework: Four Moves

Sketch these four quadrants in your inquiry notebook. Label each one with the word, the title, and the core question. These are not steps on a checklist. They are a way of seeing. Every time you consume media for the rest of your life, you can use these.

01
Origin
Who Made This?

Who made this and why does that matter?

  • Who or what organization produced this?
  • Who owns or funds this source?
  • What does this source gain from you consuming it?
  • Does this source have a history of accuracy or bias?
  • Would knowing the owner change how you read this?
02
Frame
What Did They Choose?

What did they choose to show and what did they choose to leave out?

  • What words, images, or details are emphasized?
  • What perspective or voice is missing from this story?
  • How does the headline compare to the actual content?
  • If a different outlet covered this same story, what might they show differently?
  • What would this story look like told from another point of view?
03
Target
Who Is This For?

Who is this made for and how does it want me to feel?

  • Who is the intended audience for this content?
  • What emotion does this content seem designed to create?
  • Does it want you to feel angry, afraid, proud, reassured, or something else?
  • How does it feel in your body when you consume this?
  • Are you the intended audience, or did you stumble into someone else's feed?
04
Signal
What Does This Want?

What does this want me to do, think, or believe?

  • What action, if any, does this content push you toward?
  • What belief does regular consumption of this source reinforce over time?
  • Is this informing you or is it shaping you?
  • What would a citizen who only consumed this source believe about the world?
  • After reading or watching this, what do you feel compelled to do next?
Guided Practice: Six Perspectives, One Story
Ad Fontes Media Activity (35 min)

Mr. Fussell will hand you one article. Somewhere in this room, two or three other students have a different article. Every article in this room covers the exact same news event -- but from a different place on the political and reliability spectrum, sourced from Ad Fontes Media.

Using your Four Moves framework, complete a Day 2 entry in your inquiry notebook for the article you received. Use the questions as your guide -- you do not have to answer every single one, but you should work through all four moves.

In Your Notebook -- Day 2 Entry
  • What source did you receive? Who owns or funds it?
  • What choices did this article make in how it told the story?
  • What emotion did this article seem designed to create?
  • What does this article want you to believe about this event?
  • After applying the Four Moves: how would you describe this source to someone who had never seen it?

When analysts are done, Mr. Fussell will bring the room together. You will share what you found. The same event -- six different realities. That difference is the lesson.

Before You Leave

Write one sentence in your notebook: What surprised you most about the article you received? Hold onto that surprise. You will need it.

Movement Three
Day 3: First Look
70 min
The Goal

Yesterday you practiced the Four Moves on a source Mr. Fussell chose. Today you turn the lens on yourself -- on the source you actually use. This is where the investigation gets personal.

Day 3 Notebook Entry: The First Look (50 min)

Pull up or bring in examples from your chosen media source. You are going to apply all Four Moves to it. Work through each move carefully. Use the scaffolding questions. Write everything in your inquiry notebook under Day 3: First Look.

Guiding Questions for Your Notebook
  • Apply Move 1 (Origin): What do you actually know about who made this? What did you have to look up?
  • Apply Move 2 (Frame): Find one specific example from your source where a choice was made about what to include or leave out. Describe it.
  • Apply Move 3 (Target): Be honest -- how does this source make you feel? Is that feeling useful to you as a citizen, or is it useful to someone else?
  • Apply Move 4 (Signal): If you consumed only this source for one year, what would you believe about the world?
  • What is one thing you noticed today that you had never noticed before about this source?

This is not a performance. Write what you actually think. Your inquiry notebook is your thinking space -- not a place to write what sounds good.

Think Tank Check-In (15 min)

Mr. Fussell will bring the room together for a brief analyst check-in. Each analyst shares one thing they found so far -- one observation, one surprise, one question that opened up. You are not presenting yet. You are thinking out loud with your colleagues at The Reveal. Listen to what others found. Note anything that connects to your own investigation.

Before You Leave

Write this prompt in your notebook and answer it: What pattern am I starting to see -- and what might it mean for how I stay informed? This answer will be the spine of your Day 4 work.

Movement Four
Day 4: Going Deeper
70 min
The Goal

The scaffolding comes off today. You have the framework. You have your source. You have three days of observations. Now you build your case. Today is about going deeper into what you found and consolidating it into findings you can present tomorrow.

Day 4 Notebook Entry: Building the Case (45 min)

Go back through your Day 1, Day 2, and Day 3 entries. Read what you wrote. Then use the following questions to push deeper. Write your Day 4 entry in your inquiry notebook.

  • What is the clearest pattern you have found across your investigation so far?
  • What does your source do well as a source of civic information?
  • What does your source do that concerns you as a citizen?
  • Go back to your Day 1 questions. Can you answer them now? Write your answers.
  • What would you tell a friend who uses this source every day without thinking about it?
  • What does this investigation reveal about the essential question: Who is shaping what you believe, and do you consent to it?
Choose Your Track (15 min)

Tomorrow you will present your findings to The Reveal. Today you will choose how. Read the three tracks below and decide which one fits how you think and how you communicate best. There is no wrong answer. Choose the track that will let your findings shine.

Write your chosen track in your notebook and spend the remaining time preparing for it.

Closing Entry -- Do This Last

Before you close your notebook, write this: My three strongest findings from this investigation are... These three findings are what you will build your Day 5 presentation around. If you cannot write three, keep working.

Movement Five
Day 5: The Findings
70 min
The Goal

Today The Reveal presents its findings. Every analyst has spent four days investigating a media source that real people -- maybe people in your family, in your community -- consume every day. What you found matters. Today you share it.

Before you present, you will complete your final notebook entry. Then you will present using the track you chose yesterday.

Day 5 Notebook Entry: The Verdict (15 min)

Complete this final entry in your inquiry notebook before you present. This is your closing reflection as an analyst.

  • Go back to the two questions you wrote on Day 1. Answer them now, based on what you found.
  • What is the single most important thing you discovered in this investigation?
  • Did this investigation change anything about how you will consume media going forward? Be honest.
  • Answer the essential question in your own words: Who is shaping what you believe, and do you consent to it?
Choose Your Track: Three Ways to Present
Track A
The Brief

For analysts who think best through writing. You will write a structured one-page Analyst Brief by hand, in class, in your inquiry notebook or on paper provided by Mr. Fussell. This is completed during class time -- not before, not at home.

Your Brief Must Include
  • Source Analyzed: What source did you investigate and who owns it?
  • Framing Observed: What consistent choices did this source make in how it presented information?
  • Audience and Emotion: Who is this source for and how does it want them to feel?
  • The Signal: What does regular consumption of this source build in a person over time?
  • Civic Implication: What does your investigation reveal about what it means to be an informed citizen?
Track B
The Board

For analysts who think best through visual organization. You will build a large-format Evidence Board on chart paper. This is hung on the wall. Mr. Fussell and your fellow analysts will come to your board and you will walk them through your findings.

Your Board Must Show
  • The name and ownership of your source -- clearly visible
  • At least two specific examples from your source with your Four Moves analysis applied to each
  • The pattern you identified across your investigation
  • Your answer to the essential question, in your own words, large enough to read from a distance
  • Your name and your Day 1 investigative questions -- answered
Track C
The Dispatch

For analysts who think best through speaking. You will deliver a verbal presentation with a microphone. Your presentation will be recorded using Adobe Podcast, which will generate a transcript. Mr. Fussell will assess your findings from the transcript -- not from recording quality. The mic is yours. Speak like your findings matter, because they do.

Your Dispatch Must Cover
  • Introduce your source and why you chose it
  • Walk through your three strongest findings, one at a time, with specific examples
  • Explain what pattern emerged across your investigation
  • Answer your Day 1 investigative questions out loud
  • Close by answering the essential question: Who is shaping what you believe, and do you consent to it?

After everyone presents, The Reveal convenes.

Mr. Fussell will bring the think tank together for a final collective conversation. What patterns did analysts find across different sources? What does it mean -- for this class, for this community, for citizens in Eastern North Carolina -- that the media environment looks the way it does? You came in asking a question. Today you answer it together.

Artifacts of Learning
What You Will Produce and Submit

By the end of this investigation, every analyst at The Reveal will have produced two artifacts. Both are required.

Artifact One
The Media Audit Log

Your inquiry notebook entries for all five days. This is the record of your thinking across the entire investigation. It must include:

  • Day 1: Media diet responses and your two Level 2 or 3 investigative questions
  • Day 2: Four Moves analysis of your Ad Fontes article
  • Day 3: First Look analysis of your own chosen source
  • Day 4: Deep investigation entry and your three strongest findings
  • Day 5: The Verdict closing reflection
Artifact Two
Your Day 5 Presentation

The product of your chosen track, completed and presented on Day 5. This takes one of three forms:

  • Track A (The Brief): Handwritten Analyst Brief, completed in class
  • Track B (The Board): Chart paper Evidence Board, displayed and defended
  • Track C (The Dispatch): Recorded verbal presentation, transcript submitted
